Children are expected to return back to in-person learning in the fall, are the cafeterias up to the task?

May 20, 2021

By Angel Adegbesan

Health code violations are one of the many things consumers look for in a restaurant before dining. Even a minimal violation and a less than “A” grade deters consumers from dining at a food place, however, for New York City pre-k to 12 grade students, that may not be the case.

With 1.1 million students, the New York City Department of Education (NYCDOE), through its 1800 schools, feeds more people (students and members of the community) every day than almost any other public institution in the country.

Most school cafeterias remain open even after a violation that is deemed a “public health hazard”. The cafeterias are shut down and revamped when they have “Critical” violations like pests, rodents and fleas infestation or food temperature violations.

With coronavirus vaccinations ramping up, about 70-80% of students are expected to resume in-person learning in the fall and they will most definitely be needing these school cafeterias.

In the first four months of 2021, there has been over 1,000 violations reported at more than 800 schools. Some schools have more than one violations, with one Manhattan school racking up eight different violations.

Of those violations reported, 544 received a documented health code violation. And, a quarter of those documented were in the “critical” category.

The analysis discovered that 139 schools kitchens and cafeterias either contained evidence of rodents and vermins including roaches, mice, rats and flies, or had food temperature violations, or were grossly unsanitary.

Housing

26% of Black recipients said they used a large portion of their second stimulus payment on housing versus 12% of their white counterparts.

Savings

20% of White and 25% of Asian-Americans said they saved a portion or all of their second stimulus payment versus 9% of Black and 14% of Latinx Americans.

Survival

50% of Black people and 40% of Latinx people said they were counting on a third round of stimulus payment to get by, versus 22%, the share of Whites.

The Third

According to the Census Bureau's Household Pulse Survey done in Feb. 2021, household expenses and the reduction of debt continue to be the top priorities of majority of Americans who hope to receive their third stimulus check. The survey done in the week of Feb. 17 also shows that a majority plan to use the third round of payments on food and utilities.
